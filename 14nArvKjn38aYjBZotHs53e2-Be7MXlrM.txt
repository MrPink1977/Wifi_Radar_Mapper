The Real Next Steps

Step 1 — Kill the Biggest Unknowns First

Right now there are only a few things that could actually break this project:
	1.	Wi-Fi signal access on iOS
	2.	Indoor movement tracking accuracy
	3.	Heatmap generation performance
	4.	User interaction complexity

You do tiny experiments to answer those questions.

Not a full app.

Just proof-of-concept scripts.

Example experiments:

• Can we read RSSI repeatedly while walking?
• Can we track movement from phone sensors reliably for ~30 seconds?
• Can we draw a heatmap from 50 points quickly?

These tests can be done in a weekend.

That’s how real engineering projects move forward.

⸻

Step 2 — Build the Ugliest Prototype Possible

Not a product.

A tool.

Engineers call this a spike prototype.

Imagine a super crude app that:
	1.	shows current RSSI
	2.	logs samples
	3.	lets you tap points on an image
	4.	draws colored circles

That’s it.

No login
No polish
No branding
No onboarding

Just proof the core concept works.

⸻

Step 3 — Test It in the Real World

Walk around your house.

Walk around a garage.

Walk around a warehouse.

See if the heatmap actually reflects reality.

If it does, the idea survives.

If it doesn’t, you adjust.

This stage reveals 90% of hidden problems.

⸻

Step 4 — Only Then Build the Real Product

Once the prototype proves the concept:

Then you design the real UX.

Then you polish.

Then you build the production architecture.

Skipping that step is where most ideas die.

⸻

Step 5 — Recruit a Developer the Right Way

When you talk to your lead dev friend, don’t say:

“I have an app idea.”

Say this instead:

“I built a working prototype that maps signal strength onto a floorplan. I want to turn it into a product.”

That changes the entire conversation.

Developers respect working prototypes far more than idea decks.

⸻

One More Hard Truth

The biggest killer of projects like this isn’t tech.

It’s scope creep.

Right now the core app is extremely simple:

Walk → sample signal → paint heatmap.

That’s the product.

Everything else is optional.

⸻

A Very Important Insight

The most successful apps start with a single job.

Examples:

Uber → get a ride
Shazam → identify a song
Speedtest → test internet speed

Your core job is:

“Show me the best place for my wireless devices.”

Everything in the app should serve that.
